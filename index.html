Animal Crossing: New Horizons

Animal Crossing New Horizons - A Review

A perfect microcosm in a quarantined world.

I’ve always had a sweet spot for the Animal Crossing series, but I haven’t really realised why - until recently. Back in 2020, during my final years of highschool, getting out of bed and committing to the workload was an overwhelmingly stressful experience, especially at the height of the COVID-19 pandemic. As a result, this made it even more difficult to make friends or stay in contact with already existing ones (out of the very few existing friends I already had at the time). On top of this, I didn’t really talk to many people to begin with, thanks to my awkward social tendencies. So more often than not, I finished each day feeling pretty demoralised.
As a natural response, I turned to video games as a form to escape - finding comfort in games like Stardew Valley, Super Mario Galaxy, and most notably, Animal Crossing. Whereas my school days were filled with isolation and even alienation, Animal Crossing felt like a world I could belong in. One where I was in control; one where I could have friends, write them letters and they would write back. Where the time progressed the same as outside and made it feel real. It sounds pretty ridiculous now, but to a depressed teenager, it was a much needed break.

AC:NH Launch Trailer

As a result of my retreatment into Animal Crossing throughout the peak of the pandemic, it was one of the primary games that piqued my interest regarding the topic of escapism in general. Were others doing this? Was this a documented phenomenon? How directly was this linked to my (admittedly faltering) mental health? I had so many mild (yet prominent) questions, and one way to answer them - it was time for research. Not only did this lead me down a rabbit hole of videogame academia, but also has pushed me to connect with fellow video game enthusiasts, discussing their experiences with their favourite games and the idea of residing in their own ‘personal paradise’.

So what actually is escapism?

Interestingly enough, I found that the idea of escapism through digital media, particularly video games, has been the topic of much debate for years - down to the very definition of the word "escapism". For the sake of clarity in my brief delve into the depths of video game psychology, I'll simplify the definition adopted by Andreas Hansen and Yuhui Gan in their paper An Analysis of The Impact of Escapism on Players - to define escapism as "using video games to avoid other aspects of life".
Given that this is a video game blog post and not a peer reviewed paper, I don't want to approach this with the academic rigour of one, rather as a brief discussion of the fascinating concepts mentioned in previous research that affect every single one of us gamers. One common distinction made in previous research is the difference between healthy and unhealthy escapism - which immediately fascinated me, as I felt I had experienced both in my hours upon hours of Animal Crossing. Not only was I playing to amuse myself, enjoy the game, and honestly just boost my mood - but also playing to avoid having to do anything else, as I felt I couldn't really face the world, which to some degree worsened how I felt as I felt the disconnect between me and the outside world growing every day. This feeling was - of course - backed up directly by further research in this paper, going into the concept of Internet Gaming Disorder (an aptly named concept) motivated by an escapist mentality.
In Hansen and Gan's paper, they investigate a topic dubbed the "Four Pillars of Healthy Escapism", founded upon emotional regulation, mood management, coping, and recovery. Again the research felt eerily accurate in its parallels to my personal experience throughout the height of COVID lockdowns, as they then go on to provide definitions of the concepts in the context of video games. Interestingly it seems that much of the research done into the idea of escapism specifically addresses MMOs for the associated social interaction - but as a more singleplayer focused person that recreation of social interaction hadn't really struck me before, so I'd love to see in the comments if any of you felt this way!

But why Animal Crossing?

As I previously mentioned, AC:NH came into my life at a very opportune time - when social interaction wasn't necessarily possible (and not just because of COVID, primarily the psychological impacts it had on me and my ability to socialise). So the appeal of creating and existing within a perfect little world immediately won me over.
The combination of comfy vibes and entirely self-paced gameplay not only made Animal Crossing a staple of my quarantine experience, but also a pillar of my ups and downs in mental health. The journey I went on while sitting at home playing video games was remarkably significant to me, from playing to boost my mood to unintentionally isolating myself from the rest of the world, it was all worth it to be here on AAOB writing articles about my favourite games. Thank you, and I can't wait to read about your game escapism experiences in the comments below.
